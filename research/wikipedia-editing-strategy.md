## Executive Summary

This document provides a strategic framework for improving monday.com's Wikipedia presence while maintaining strict compliance with Wikipedia's policies. The research covers account strategy, detection mechanisms, best practices, content strategy, service providers, and a detailed roadmap for monday.com.

**Key Findings:**
- Wikipedia allows paid editing but requires **mandatory disclosure** (WP:PAID)
- Direct editing is strongly discouraged; **talk page proposals** are the gold standard
- Account reputation building takes **6-12 months minimum**
- Buying accounts is prohibited and carries severe risks
- The recommended approach is **80%+ community contributions / <20% self-serving edits**
- monday.com already has a Wikipedia page with strong notability

## 1. Wikipedia Account Strategy

### 1.1 Creating a Wikipedia Editor Account

Creating an account is straightforward, but building credibility is a long-term investment:

**Account Creation Process:**
- Navigate to Wikipedia and click "Create account"
- Choose a personal username (NOT "Monday.com" or "MondayComTeam")
- Corporate/organizational usernames are **explicitly prohibited** under WP:USERNAME
- Shared accounts are not permitted - each editor must use their own account

**Critical Rule:** Wikipedia's username policy strictly prohibits usernames that represent companies or groups. Accounts must be personal, even if the editor is employed by or affiliated with monday.com.

### 1.2 Account Reputation Building

**Timeline:** Building a credible editing history takes **6-12 months minimum**, though some consultants recommend **12-24 months** of consistent contribution before making any edits related to your employer.

**What Contributes to Reputation:**

1. **Edit Count & Consistency**
   - A "record of many edits over years, mostly good, will bring much credibility"
   - Regular contributions over months/years demonstrate commitment
   - Quality matters more than quantity

2. **Adherence to Core Policies**
   - **WP:NPOV** (Neutral Point of View): Present information without bias
   - **WP:V** (Verifiability): All claims backed by reliable sources
   - **WP:NOR** (No Original Research): No unpublished analysis or novel ideas

3. **Community Engagement**
   - Constructive participation in Talk page discussions
   - Respectful collaboration with other editors
   - Demonstrating good judgment on content decisions

4. **Types of Contributions**
   - Fixing typos, grammar, formatting (minor edits)
   - Adding well-sourced information to articles
   - Fixing citation formatting
   - Expanding stub articles
   - Participating in consensus-building discussions

**What Destroys Credibility:**
- "Stupid nastiness" toward other editors
- Violating WP:NPOV or WP:V
- Promotional editing
- Edit warring (repeatedly reverting changes without discussion)
- Undisclosed conflicts of interest

### 1.3 Can You Buy Accounts with Existing Reputation?

**Short Answer:** Yes, they are available for purchase, but this is **strictly prohibited** and **extremely risky**.

**Availability:**
- Multiple websites openly sell "aged" or "verified" Wikipedia accounts
- Prices vary based on account age, edit history, and permissions
- Some sellers claim accounts have "established credibility" or "advanced permissions"

**Risks of Purchased Accounts:**

1. **Severe Policy Violation**
   - Direct violation of Wikipedia's Terms of Service
   - Considered unethical and a breach of community trust
   - Falls under "sockpuppetry" (deceptive use of multiple accounts)

2. **Detection & Consequences**
   - **CheckUser tool** can link multiple accounts to the same individual/IP
   - All related accounts can be **indefinitely blocked**
   - IP addresses and editing patterns are exposed
   - Site-wide ban for repeat offenders

3. **Reputational Damage**
   - Public exposure of the practice
   - All edits from purchased accounts may be reverted
   - Articles created through such accounts may be deleted
   - Permanent "badge of shame" on company Wikipedia page

4. **Case Study: Orangemoody (2015)**
   - CheckUsers exposed **381 sockpuppet accounts**
   - Involved in an extortion scheme charging businesses to create promotional articles
   - All accounts permanently blocked
   - Significant negative media coverage

**Recommendation:** **NEVER buy Wikipedia accounts.** The risks far outweigh any perceived benefits, and detection is increasingly sophisticated.

### 1.4 Disclosed Corporate Account vs. Anonymous Editing

**The Gold Standard: Disclosed Personal Account with COI Declaration**

Wikipedia does not allow "corporate accounts" in the traditional sense, but it does allow (and strongly prefers) disclosed editing:

**Disclosed Approach (RECOMMENDED):**
- Use a **personal username** (e.g., "JohnSmithMondayTeam")
- Add a clear **disclosure on your user page**: "I am employed by monday.com and edit in accordance with WP:PAID and WP:COI"
- Declare COI on talk pages when proposing changes
- Include disclosure in edit summaries when appropriate

**Anonymous/Undisclosed Approach (HIGH RISK):**
- Editing without disclosure violates WP:PAID
- If discovered, leads to account blocks and content removal
- Company IP addresses are publicly visible - anonymous editing from office IPs is easily traced
- Creates reputational risk when inevitably discovered

**Mandatory Disclosure Requirements (WP:PAID):**

The Wikimedia Foundation's Terms of Use **legally require** paid editors to disclose:
1. Their employer
2. Their client (if applicable)
3. Their affiliation with the subject

This disclosure must appear in at least one of these locations:
- User page
- Article talk page
- Edit summary

**Consequences of Non-Disclosure:**
- Indefinite blocking of account
- Deletion of contributed content
- Potential cease-and-desist letter from Wikimedia Foundation
- Severe reputational damage

### 1.5 Wikipedia's Paid Editing Policy (WP:PAID, WP:COI)

**WP:PAID (Paid-contribution disclosure):**

Paid editing is **not prohibited**, but it is **strictly regulated**:

✅ **Allowed:**
- Editing Wikipedia while being compensated, IF properly disclosed
- Proposing changes to articles where you have a financial interest
- Consulting with companies on Wikipedia strategy

❌ **Prohibited:**
- **Paid advocacy**: Being compensated to promote or disparage a subject
- Editing without disclosure of employer/client/affiliation
- Making direct edits to articles where you have a financial interest (strongly discouraged)
- Guaranteeing specific outcomes (article creation, content retention, etc.)

**WP:COI (Conflict of Interest):**

A conflict of interest exists when editing about:
- Yourself, family, or friends
- Your employer or clients
- Your financial interests
- Any topic where you have a personal or professional stake

**COI Best Practices:**
1. **Disclose the conflict** on your user page and relevant talk pages
2. **Avoid direct editing** of articles where COI exists
3. **Propose changes on talk pages** using {{edit COI}} template
4. **Let neutral editors implement** approved changes
5. **Focus on improving sourcing** rather than adding promotional content

**The Wikimedia Foundation's Stance:**
- Supports community investigations into undisclosed paid editing
- Issues cease-and-desist letters to firms violating terms of use
- Does NOT endorse or offer paid editing services
- Warns that many paid editing services are scams

## 2. Detection & Risk

### 2.1 How Wikipedia Detects Paid/Corporate Editing

Wikipedia employs multiple detection methods, both automated and human:

**1. CheckUser Tool**
- Available to a select group of trusted administrators
- Reveals IP addresses associated with user accounts
- Shows all edits from a particular IP address or range
- Can link multiple accounts to the same individual/organization
- Used to uncover "sockpuppet" networks

**2. ORES (Objective Revision Evaluation Service)**
- Machine learning service that evaluates edit quality
- Identifies potentially damaging edits automatically
- Effective for detecting low-quality contributions
- May not catch sophisticated paid editors

**3. Automated Bots**
- Approved by Bot Approval Group (BAG) committee
- Implement mechanisms to detect paid editing patterns
- Specific detection methods not publicly disclosed (to prevent circumvention)

**4. Human Investigation ("Snooping")**
- Experienced volunteer editors actively monitor suspicious activity
- Look for patterns: promotional tone, multiple accounts editing same articles, unusual editing patterns
- Investigate connections between usernames and external websites
- Cross-reference with LinkedIn profiles, company websites, job postings

**5. External Evidence**
- Job postings seeking Wikipedia editors
- Company website identities matching Wikipedia usernames
- Public statements about Wikipedia editing efforts
- Leaked emails (as in the Bell Pottinger / Gupta scandal)

**6. Behavioral Patterns**
- Multiple new accounts editing the same article
- Overly promotional language
- Removal of negative information
- Addition of content with only self-referencing sources
- Editing only articles related to one company/industry
- Timing patterns (all edits during business hours in one timezone)

### 2.2 What Happens When Caught

**Immediate Consequences:**

1. **Account Blocks and Bans**
   - Indefinite blocking of the offending account(s)
   - Blocking of all related sockpuppet accounts
   - Potential site-wide ban for repeat offenders
   - IP range blocks (affecting entire organizations)

2. **Content Removal**
   - Deletion of articles created through undisclosed paid editing
   - Reversion of biased or promotional edits
   - Removal of unreliably sourced content

3. **Article Protection**
   - Articles may be "protected" (locked from editing by certain user groups)
   - Can last from days to indefinite periods
   - Makes future editing much more difficult

4. **Editing Restrictions**
   - Topic bans (prohibited from editing certain subjects)
   - Interaction bans (can't interact with specific editors)
   - One-account-only restrictions

**Long-term Consequences:**

1. **Reputational Damage**
   - Negative media coverage (as with Bell Pottinger, Wiki-PR)
   - Public exposure of the manipulation attempt
   - Permanent record on Wikipedia and in news articles
   - Damage to brand trust and credibility

2. **Legal Action**
   - Cease-and-desist letters from Wikimedia Foundation
   - Potential trademark violation claims
   - Public statements from Wikipedia co-founder Jimmy Wales (as with Bell Pottinger)

3. **"Badge of Shame"**
   - Critical information may be added to the company's Wikipedia page about the editing scandal
   - Difficult to remove due to WP:NPOV and WP:V requirements
   - Can persist for years

## 3. Best Practices — The 80/20 Rule

### 3.1 The Community Contribution Ratio

**Recommended Ratio: 80%+ community contributions / <20% self-serving edits**

This ratio is widely recommended by Wikipedia consultants and is based on the principle of establishing credibility before making edits related to your financial interests.

**Why This Ratio?**

1. **Demonstrates Good Faith**
   - Shows commitment to Wikipedia's mission, not just self-interest
   - Builds trust with other editors who review your work

2. **Establishes Credibility**
   - Other editors learn you understand and follow Wikipedia policies
   - Creates a positive reputation that provides benefit of the doubt

3. **Reduces Scrutiny**
   - Accounts with diverse editing history are less suspicious
   - Pure promotional accounts are immediately flagged

4. **Provides Learning Opportunity**
   - Editing unrelated topics teaches Wikipedia norms and culture
   - Helps you understand what makes good Wikipedia content

**Important Caveat:** Even with an 80/20 ratio, **disclosure of COI is still mandatory** when making any self-serving edits.

### 3.2 Building Credibility Before Self-Interested Edits

**Recommended Warm-Up Period: 6-12 months**

**Phase 1: Months 1-3 - Pure Community Contributions**
- Make ZERO edits related to your company, industry, or competitors
- Focus on topics you're genuinely interested in or knowledgeable about
- Aim for 50-100 edits minimum
- Learn Wikipedia culture and norms

**Phase 2: Months 4-6 - Industry Context Building**
- Begin editing articles in your broader industry (not your company)
- Contribute to industry terminology, technology, or methodology articles
- Add citations to industry studies and research
- Establish yourself as knowledgeable about the field

**Phase 3: Months 7-12 - Gradual Self-Related Contributions**
- Make occasional edits to tangentially related topics
- If you must edit your company's article, START with talk page proposals
- Never make more than 1-2 self-serving edits for every 8-10 community contributions

**Long-term Maintenance:**
- Continue 80/20 ratio indefinitely
- Regular community contributions maintain credibility
- Prevents account from looking like a single-purpose promotional account

### 3.3 Types of Community Contributions Most Valued

**High-Value Contributions:**

1. **Fixing Citations and References**
   - Replacing dead links with archived versions
   - Adding proper citation formatting
   - Adding reliable sources to unsourced claims
   - Converting inline external links to proper footnotes

2. **Minor Cleanup Edits**
   - Typo and grammar corrections
   - Formatting consistency
   - Fixing broken templates
   - Standardizing date formats, units, etc.

3. **Expanding Stub Articles**
   - Articles marked as "stubs" need expansion
   - Adding well-sourced content to short articles shows genuine contribution
   - Choose topics you know well or can research properly

4. **Adding Missing Information**
   - Filling gaps in articles using reliable sources
   - Adding historical context
   - Updating outdated information
   - Adding relevant images (properly licensed)

5. **Talk Page Participation**
   - Constructive discussion on article improvements
   - Helping resolve disputes through consensus
   - Providing insight on sources or content questions

6. **Reverting Vandalism**
   - Fixing obvious vandalism (though be careful not to engage in edit wars)
   - Reporting persistent vandals

7. **WikiProject Participation**
   - Join WikiProjects related to your interests
   - Collaborate on improving articles within a topic area
   - Shows long-term commitment to Wikipedia

**Moderate-Value Contributions:**
- Copy-editing for style and clarity
- Adding internal wiki-links to relevant articles
- Categorizing articles properly
- Creating disambiguation pages

**Low-Value / Potentially Suspicious:**
- Only making tiny edits to avoid looking inactive
- Editing only articles that might benefit your employer indirectly
- Mass-editing with automated tools (requires approval)

### 3.4 What "Self-Serving" Means in Practice

**Clearly Self-Serving (Requires Disclosure):**
- Editing your own company's article
- Editing articles about your company's products/services
- Editing articles about company executives
- Removing criticism of your company
- Adding positive information about your company
- Editing competitor articles

**Potentially Self-Serving (Gray Area, Disclosure Recommended):**
- Editing industry articles to add your company as an example
- Adding industry data that happens to favor your company's narrative
- Editing technology/methodology articles that relate to your product category
- Editing biographical articles of people associated with your company

**Generally Acceptable (No COI):**
- Editing articles completely unrelated to your company or industry
- Fixing factual errors anywhere on Wikipedia
- Adding properly cited information to any article
- Reverting vandalism
- Participating in policy discussions

## 4. Content Strategy

### 4.1 Safest Types of Edits for Companies

Even when disclosed as a paid editor, some types of edits are safer and more likely to be accepted:

#### **1. Adding Reliable Third-Party Citations**

✅ **SAFE - High Success Rate**

**What to do:**
- Find existing unsourced claims that happen to be true
- Add citations to reliable, independent third-party sources
- Focus on factual, non-promotional information

**Example:**
- Article states: "Monday.com raised significant funding in 2019"
- Add citation to: TechCrunch, Bloomberg, or other independent news source

**Why it's safe:**
- Improves Wikipedia's core mission (verifiability)
- Uses independent sources (not company PR)
- Doesn't add new promotional content
- Easy to verify

**Best Practices:**
- Use **WP:RS** (Reliable Sources): mainstream media, academic journals, government documents
- Avoid: company press releases, company blog, marketing materials
- Format citations properly using Wikipedia templates
- Don't add citations to promotional or biased claims

#### **2. Fixing Dead Links**

✅ **VERY SAFE - Universally Appreciated**

**What to do:**
- Identify dead/broken links in citations
- Find archived versions (via Wayback Machine)
- Replace dead links with working archived versions
- Or find alternative reliable sources for the same information

**Why it's safe:**
- Pure maintenance, no content change
- Widely appreciated by Wikipedia community
- Demonstrates good faith
- No promotional aspect

**Tools:**
- Wayback Machine (archive.org)
- Wikipedia's {{dead link}} template
- IABot (InternetArchiveBot) does this automatically, but you can help

#### **3. Adding Industry Data/Benchmarks**

⚠️ **MODERATE RISK - Requires Careful Sourcing**

**What to do:**
- Add industry statistics, market size data, or trends
- Use data from reputable third-party sources
- Ensure neutrality (don't cherry-pick data favoring your company)

**Safe Sources:**
- Gartner, Forrester, IDC (market research firms)
- Government statistics (e.g., Bureau of Labor Statistics)
- Academic research and peer-reviewed journals
- Mainstream business media (WSJ, Bloomberg, Forbes)

**Risky Sources:**
- Company-commissioned research
- Industry associations with clear bias
- Self-published surveys
- Data that only appears in your company's materials

**Example - Safe:**
"The global work management software market was valued at $X billion in 2024 according to Gartner."

**Example - Risky:**
"Monday.com is the leading work management platform according to a 2024 survey." (even if true, sounds promotional)

**Best Practices:**
- Propose on talk page first if you have COI
- Ensure data is presented neutrally
- Don't use data selectively to favor your company
- Cite independent, reputable sources only

#### **4. Expanding Stub Articles in Your Industry**

⚠️ **MODERATE RISK - Depends on Neutrality**

**What to do:**
- Find "stub" articles (very short articles) in your industry
- Expand with well-sourced, neutral information
- Avoid mentioning your company unless truly notable in that context

**Why it's valuable:**
- Shows expertise in the field
- Contributes to Wikipedia's coverage
- Can establish you as a knowledgeable editor

**Risks:**
- Must be scrupulously neutral
- Don't use as backdoor to promote your company
- Other editors will scrutinize additions from disclosed paid editors

**Best Practices:**
- Choose articles genuinely related to your expertise
- Cite diverse, independent sources
- Don't mention your company unless absolutely relevant and supported by sources
- Propose major additions on talk page first

### 4.2 Types of Edits That Trigger Alarms

#### **1. Removing Negative Information**

🚨 **VERY HIGH RISK - Almost Always Rejected**

**What it looks like:**
- Deleting criticism sections
- Removing controversy subsections
- Deleting mentions of lawsuits, scandals, or problems
- Toning down negative language without source support

**Why it triggers alarms:**
- Obvious COI motivation
- Violates WP:NPOV (requires representing all significant viewpoints)
- Frequently attempted by companies, so editors are vigilant

**When removal IS acceptable:**
- Content is unsourced and about living persons (WP:BLP)
- Content violates Wikipedia policies (original research, unreliable source)
- Content is genuinely defamatory and false
- Must be discussed on talk page first with clear policy justification

**Proper approach if negative content is problematic:**
1. Check if content is properly sourced
2. If unsourced, you MAY remove it (especially for WP:BLP violations)
3. If sourced, propose on talk page with specific policy concerns
4. Offer better sources that provide more context
5. Never just delete because it's unflattering

#### **2. Adding Promotional Language**

🚨 **VERY HIGH RISK - Immediate Reversion Likely**

**What it looks like:**
- "Leading," "innovative," "revolutionary" without attribution
- Superlatives: "best," "most advanced," "world-class"
- Marketing buzzwords: "cutting-edge," "next-generation," "seamless"
- Sales language: "solutions," "empower," "transform"
- Peacock terms: "prestigious," "renowned," "acclaimed" (without sourcing)

**Examples of promotional language:**
- ❌ "Monday.com is the leading work management platform that empowers teams worldwide"
- ✅ "Monday.com is a work management platform founded in 2012"
- ❌ "The innovative and user-friendly interface revolutionizes how teams collaborate"
- ✅ "The platform offers customizable workflows and project tracking features"

**Why it triggers alarms:**
- Violates WP:NPOV (Wikipedia's "voice" should be neutral)
- Obvious indication of undisclosed paid editing or COI
- Wikipedia editors are trained to spot promotional tone

**How to fix promotional content:**
- Use neutral, encyclopedic language
- Attribute opinions to sources: "Forbes described the platform as 'user-friendly'"
- Stick to verifiable facts
- Let the facts speak for themselves

#### **3. Editing Competitor Articles**

🚨 **EXTREME RISK - Highly Scrutinized**

**What it looks like:**
- Editing articles about direct competitors
- Adding negative information to competitor pages
- Removing positive information from competitor pages
- Making your company look better by comparison

**Why it's extremely risky:**
- Obvious COI
- Likely to start edit wars
- Other companies will retaliate
- Wikipedia community will notice the pattern
- Can lead to topic bans

**Case Example:**
- The edit war that led to the Ethical Wiki case study involved competitors editing each other's pages

**When it might be acceptable:**
- Fixing genuinely incorrect factual information (with reliable sources)
- Proposing on talk page first
- Full disclosure of your affiliation
- Must be prepared for intense scrutiny

**Best Practice:**
- **Generally avoid competitor articles entirely**
- If you must address competitor information, do it in industry overview articles, not their specific page
- Focus on improving your own company's article, not undermining competitors

#### **4. Adding Only Self-Referencing Sources**

🚨 **HIGH RISK - Signals Lack of Notability or Promotional Intent**

**What it looks like:**
- All citations point to company blog, press releases, or website
- No third-party coverage cited
- Primary sources used for secondary claims

**Why it triggers alarms:**
- Suggests company isn't notable enough for Wikipedia (fails WP:N)
- Indicates inability to find independent coverage
- Violates WP:V and WP:IRS (Independent Reliable Sources)

**Wikipedia's source hierarchy:**
1. **Best:** Independent, third-party reliable sources (mainstream media, academic journals)
2. **Acceptable for basic facts:** Company website (for founding date, headquarters, basic info)
3. **Avoid:** Company blog, press releases, marketing materials (for anything beyond basic facts)
4. **Never acceptable:** Social media, self-published sources, promotional content

**Proper approach:**
- Use company sources ONLY for basic, uncontroversial facts (headquarters location, founding date)
- Use independent sources for anything that establishes importance, impact, or reception
- Mix of source types shows healthy article development

**Example - Good sourcing:**
- Founding date → Company About page ✅
- Funding rounds → TechCrunch, Bloomberg ✅
- Market position → Gartner report ✅
- Product capabilities → Independent review in PCMag ✅
- Awards → Award-giving organization's website ✅

**Example - Poor sourcing:**
- Everything → Company press releases ❌

## 5. External Service Providers

### 5.1 Legitimate Wikipedia Editing Services

Yes, legitimate Wikipedia editing services exist. They operate within Wikipedia's rules by:
- Fully disclosing paid editing
- Proposing changes on talk pages rather than direct editing
- Using only reliable, independent sources
- Maintaining WP:NPOV
- NOT guaranteeing outcomes

These are called **"white hat" Wikipedia consultants**.

### 5.2 White Hat vs. Black Hat Services

**White Hat Services (LEGITIMATE):**

✅ Characteristics:
- Transparent disclosure of paid editing
- Ethics-first approach
- Propose changes on talk pages for community review
- Rigorous research and reliable sourcing
- Deep understanding of Wikipedia policies
- Do NOT guarantee article creation or content retention
- Work WITH the Wikipedia community

✅ Examples:
- **Beutler Ink** (founded by William Beutler, long-time Wikipedia editor)
- **WhiteHatWiki**
- **Ethical Wiki**
- **WikiBlueprint**

**Black Hat Services (SCAMS/UNETHICAL):**

❌ Characteristics:
- Make unrealistic promises (guaranteed article creation)
- Claim "insider access" to Wikipedia admins
- Bypass Wikipedia policies
- Make direct edits without disclosure
- Use sockpuppet accounts
- Don't disclose COI
- Often lead to content removal and bans

❌ Red Flags:
- "We guarantee your Wikipedia page will be created"
- "We have special relationships with Wikipedia editors"
- "We can get your page published in 48 hours"
- "We can remove negative information permanently"
- Extremely cheap prices (too good to be true)
- No mention of Wikipedia policies or COI

**Warning:** The Wikimedia Foundation explicitly states:
- It does NOT endorse paid editing services
- Many companies advertising Wikipedia services are dishonest and incompetent
- No service can guarantee Wikipedia outcomes
- Be cautious of solicitations claiming to be from Wikipedia

### 5.3 What Legitimate Services Charge
